It is intentionally principle-based: this is our north-star guidance for how we lead, decide, and partner. It does not duplicate detailed or measurable commitments already tracked elsewhere.

“We are not leaving Day 2 with more words. We are leaving with one shared leadership posture: aligned, accountable, and focused on customer value.”
Product Alignment Synthesis

What We Took from the Clarissa Discussion

The Clarissa session reinforced that engineering and product win together or miss together. The key takeaways below define how we should work moving forward.

Shared Understanding

Direction Must Be Explicit

Leaders should be able to articulate product intent clearly and consistently. Team alignment starts with shared language around outcomes, tradeoffs, and priorities.

Partnership Standard

Engineering Is a Product Partner, Not an Intake Function

The expectation is active collaboration: challenge assumptions early, shape options together, and avoid handoff-style behavior that fragments ownership.

Execution Posture

Outcome Clarity Beats Feature Throughput

Velocity only matters if it moves customer and business outcomes. We prioritize work that has a clear reason, a visible impact path, and a deliberate finish line.

Leadership Behavior

Healthy Tension Is a Requirement

Constructive disagreement between product and engineering is not failure. It is how quality decisions are made when assumptions, risks, and constraints are surfaced early.

Personal Vision Synthesis

Themes Across Team Vision & Goals Statements

Across the round robin, a consistent pattern emerged: leaders are aiming for higher clarity, stronger ownership, and better system-level impact. These are the cross-cutting themes that unify individual statements.

  • From intention to evidence: leaders want to demonstrate impact through observable outcomes, not narrative alone.
  • From broad ambition to focused execution: fewer active priorities, completed with higher quality and better follow-through.
  • From individual heroics to team enablement: grow people, distribute capability, and avoid concentration of critical ownership.
  • From reactive pressure to deliberate leadership: protect capacity for prevention, architecture, and quality before incidents force action.
  • From siloed progress to first-team behavior: solve cross-team issues as shared leadership work, not local optimization.
General Team Commitment

Guiding Principles We Commit to as a Team

These principles are intended to stay stable across quarter transitions. They provide a consistent leadership standard even as specific goals evolve.

1) Customer and clinician value first

We prioritize decisions that improve real user outcomes and workflow effectiveness, not just internal convenience.

2) Clarity before speed

We move fast when ownership, intent, and tradeoffs are explicit. Ambiguity is resolved early, not carried downstream.

3) Evidence over narrative

We do not rely on anecdote as proof of progress. Claims should be grounded in observable signals and repeatable learning.

4) Shared accountability

Leadership commitments are team commitments. We help each other close loops, not just report status.

5) Cross-functional partnership by default

Product, engineering, and security alignment is part of delivery work—not an afterthought or escalation-only activity.

6) Sustainable excellence

We reject heroics as an operating model. We build systems, habits, and environments where quality is repeatable.

Post-Day 2 Carry Forward

How We Keep This Alive in Weekly Leadership Work

These are lightweight operating agreements to keep the Day 2 intent active between quarterlies, without replacing formal planning or tracking artifacts.

Operating Agreements

  • Start key alignment conversations with context and intended outcome, not status narration.
  • Call out tradeoffs explicitly when priorities conflict; avoid hidden assumptions.
  • Use peer challenge to improve decisions early, before execution hardens.
  • Protect focus by limiting parallel priority load and finishing what we start.
  • Treat post-incident and post-delivery learning as leadership responsibilities, not optional cleanup.

Team Commitment Statement

As a leadership team, we commit to lead as one: aligned with product intent, accountable to each other, and grounded in evidence. We will hold a high bar with clarity and respect, develop leaders around us, and convert strategic direction into reliable execution that improves outcomes for customers and teams.
