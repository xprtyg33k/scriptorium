Security Posture and Engineering Alignment  —  Dan

Director of Information Security  ·  11:00 – 11:50 AM
Guest

Security and engineering velocity are in real tension. HIPAA constraints shape tool selection. Pen testing adds time to releases. The AWS contractor access revocation in April 2026 disrupted multiple teams. This session names those tensions directly, distinguishes the non-negotiables from the negotiable, and leaves with concrete joint commitments. Engineering cannot treat security as someone else’s problem — and security cannot afford to block engineering without a faster alternative path.

Questions for this conversation

  • Where is security limiting velocity and what’s the faster path we haven’t found yet?
  • What are the absolute non-negotiables for Q3 — compliance, pen testing, HITRUST?
  • How do we handle the next contractor access revocation without multi-team disruption?
  • Which AI tools currently lack BAA coverage, and what’s the approval path?
  • What should engineering leaders do before adopting a new tool to avoid retroactive compliance problems?

What this produces

  • 2-3 joint commitments with owners from both security and engineering
  • Shared understanding of what is fixed vs. what is moveable this quarter
  • An agreed path for contractor access provisioning that serves both security and speed
  • Clarity on AI tool HIPAA/BAA status — feeds into the AI governance decisions from Day 1

The team agreed to standardize AI tooling and establish cost and governance guardrails. Several tools in active use may not have HIPAA/BAA coverage. 8:45 – 10:00

Personal Vision and Goals: Round Robin

The drafts from Monday afternoon get shared this morning. Each leader reads their vision, their quarterly goals, and their honest gap. The group offers genuine challenge — not affirmation. This is not a performance review exercise. It is a leadership growth exercise, done in public, with the team as witness.

Vision and Goals Sharing

Round robin  ·  5-7 min per person + 2 min group challenge  ·  Guillermo goes first
Vision

Each leader shares four things: their vision of who they want to be as a leader by the next onsite, 2-3 specific goals for Q3 and the remainder of 2026, how that vision connects to character and core values, and — the hardest one — the gap between who they want to be and who they are right now. After each share, the group offers one piece of genuine challenge. Not reassurance. A real question or observation.

What each share covers

  • Vision: Who do you want to be as a leader at the Q3 onsite? Not what you’ll have done — who you’ll have become.
  • Goals: 2-3 specific, measurable goals for Q3 and H2 2026. These connect to collective goals.
  • Connection: How does this vision reflect your core values and the leader you believe this team needs?
  • Gap: Where are you not yet the leader you described? Name it plainly.

What this produces

  • A completed personal vision and goals document per leader — a living artifact revisited monthly
  • Peer accountability: the group has witnessed each person’s gap and commitment
  • A shared understanding of who each leader is trying to become and what they are working on
  • Input for the team goals session: individual commitments should connect to team-level goals
“A good leader is a visionary, an exemplar, and a mentor. When you describe your vision — ask yourself: does it include all three?”
12:45 – 1:45

Whiteboard Goals: From Aspiration to Commitment

This is the team’s commitment artifact for Q3 and H2 2026. The session starts open and generative, then narrows. Every goal that makes the final list must survive five questions. Goals that can’t are either reframed or moved to the parking lot. What remains is what the team is held to.

Four-Phase Goals Session

Brainstorm → Cluster → Refine → Commit  ·  Standing at whiteboard
Goals

Phase 1 is open: write every goal, aspiration, and outcome anyone wants to see the team achieve. No filtering yet. Volume is the point. Phase 2 clusters and prioritizes through a vote — the top 8-10 survive. Phase 3 is where the real work happens: the facilitator asks the same five questions about each surviving goal until it is specific enough to act on and honest enough to measure. Phase 4 locks the final list with owners and deadlines.

The five refinement questions

  • Measurable: How would we know if we achieved this? What’s the number or signal?
  • Owned: Who owns making this happen? Not “the team” — a person.
  • Time-bound: By when? End of Q3? A specific date?
  • Realistic: Is this within our control or influence this quarter?
  • Specific: Is this specific enough that someone new to the team would know exactly what to do?

Goal candidates from Day 1

  • AI operational metrics baseline — LoC + velocity shift, owner TBD
  • Team identity document — one-page, shareable, done before Q3 onsite
  • EMR Industrial Design initiative — named, staffed, and scoped
  • AI capacity reallocation model adopted across all teams
  • Security / engineering friction items from Dan’s session this morning
  • Product / engineering commitments from Clarissa’s session this morning
Standard to hold

A goal that cannot answer “how would we know if we achieved this?” does not belong on the commitment list. Some aspirations are legitimate aspirations. They should be named as such, not forced into quarterly form. Better an honest aspiration than a vague commitment nobody tracks.

2:30 – 3:00

Commitments, Integration, and Close

Every onsite that does not produce explicit, named, distributed commitments becomes a conversation that evaporates by the following Monday. The closing round produces one specific thing per leader, distributed accountability via peer partners, and a confirmed follow-through cadence before anyone leaves the room.

1

One 30-Day Commitment

One specific action each leader will take in the next 30 days. Not a direction — a behavior or deliverable that can be verified at the next monthly check-in.

“I will have an AI operational metrics baseline ready to share at the next SEL Weekly Hour, owner: [name].”
2

One Behavior to Stop

One specific pattern to retire. Not a platitude — a named trigger and a named behavior. Ask yourself: what would I be embarrassed to still be doing by the Q3 onsite?

“I will stop presenting wins without before/after evidence. If I don’t have the data, I’ll say so and name the gap.”
3

Accountability Partner

Name one person from this group who will check in on your commitment weekly for 30 days. Choose someone who will genuinely challenge you, not just affirm you. Pairs are confirmed before everyone leaves.

“Rafael will check in with me every Friday — one question: did you do the thing you committed to?”
