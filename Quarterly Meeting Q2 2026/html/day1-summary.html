Discussions & Emerging Direction

Key Insights from Day 1

These are the ideas and signals that surfaced beyond the prepared win presentations — the high-value conversations that the team needs to carry forward into Day 2 and beyond.

01
Team Identity
The team cannot socialize what it cannot summarize — a team identity document is needed

A recurring pattern surfaced: when team members or external stakeholders ask who this team is and what it stands for, there is no single, sharp, shareable answer. The individual manifestos from prior onsites capture rich personal perspectives, and the Operating Model captures structure — but what is missing is a concise team identity statement that can be handed to a new engineer, shared with a cross-functional partner, or referenced in a leadership brief.

This is not about creating a slogan. It is about distilling what the team has built over two years of deliberate development into something that is portable and clear. Who is this team? What do they believe? What standard do they hold? The team has the answers — they are in the room — but the artifact does not yet exist in a form that travels without the people who built it.

Carry Forward Draft a one-page team identity summary before the Q3 onsite. Should cover: who we are, what we believe, how we lead, and what we are building toward. Intended audience: new engineers, cross-functional partners, and senior leadership.
02
Product-Engineering Opportunity  ·  Raised by Scott Seely
There is an “Industrial Designer” opportunity in the EMR that engineering should name and pursue

Scott Seely surfaced a compelling framing: there is an untapped role — similar to what an Industrial Designer provides in physical product development — that involves restructuring the features and behaviors of the EMR with the explicit goal of raising the utility function for the end user. This is distinct from feature adding and distinct from bug fixing. It is intentional experience re-architecture centered on how users actually use the product versus how it was designed to be used.

The premise is that the EMR has accumulated functionality over many years without a corresponding investment in the coherence, usability, and ergonomic design of the overall experience. A focused effort on this — informed by VOC data, clinic visits (as Isaac’s team is beginning to do), and behavioral analysis — could generate meaningful retention and satisfaction improvements that are tied to measurable utility outcomes rather than feature count.

This connects directly to product/engineering partnership and to the 2026 EMR retention target (92% → 97%). If the utility function of the product goes up, retention follows. Engineering leadership is well positioned to name and drive this effort, even if it requires pulling in product and design as collaborators.

Carry Forward Bring this framing into the Tuesday product alignment conversation with Clarissa. Explore whether there is appetite to define and staff an “EMR Industrial Design” workstream as a named Q3/H2 initiative. Identify an owner who would champion this work.
03
AI Adoption Measurement
The team reached consensus: operational data must prove AI impact, and Lines of Code is the starting point

There is a growing and urgent need — reached by consensus in today’s discussion — to move AI adoption measurement from belief and narrative to operational evidence. The current state is: teams know AI tooling is increasing speed and reducing burden, but this is not being systematically recorded in a way that makes impact visible to the organization, leadership, or the CTO.

The team agreed to begin with Lines of Code (LoC) as a leading indicator of AI-assisted development activity. While LoC is an imperfect measure of engineering output on its own, it functions as a useful operational signal of AI acceleration when contextualized with sprint velocity, time-to-completion, and team size — and it is immediately accessible from existing tooling. It is a start, not the destination.

The goal is to establish a before/after baseline that lets the team say, concretely: “Since adopting [tool] in [month], the team’s output profile has shifted in the following measurable ways.” This type of evidence is exactly what the 2026 OKR for AI Scribe adoption (5% → 35% of clinics) and the AI Center of Excellence ambition will demand.

Carry Forward Define and assign ownership of an AI operational metrics baseline. Minimum: LoC per developer week over the last two quarters, correlated with AI tool adoption timeline. Target: a reportable metric by the next monthly SEL meeting. Owner to be confirmed during Day 2 goals session.
04
AI Efficiency & Value Model  ·  Damien Evans / OnePacs
AI-enabled capacity is creating a QA bottleneck — and the right response is a holistic value model, not raw speed

The OnePacs team is generating a measurable increase in development throughput through AI-assisted work. This increased output is now creating a downstream bottleneck at QA — which is surfacing because QA capacity did not scale proportionally when development velocity did. This is a predictable and healthy signal: it means the throughput increase is real, not illusory.

Damien articulated a response that deserves to be the team’s model: rather than treating the found capacity as an opportunity to go faster indefinitely, the OnePacs team has adopted a holistic view of value delivery. The definition of “what the team produces” has shifted from raw development output to full-cycle value delivery. In practice this means the capacity freed up by AI is being redistributed across:

  • Supporting QA to prevent bottlenecks from blocking flow
  • Mentoring and coaching less experienced engineers on the team
  • Shift-left quality practices — catching issues earlier in the development cycle
  • Technical debt and foundational improvements — work that increases the team’s long-term capacity

The DICOM server rewrite is the proof point: a project that was previously unreachable given bandwidth constraints was completed because AI found the capacity. This is the right accountability model for the whole team to adopt: freed capacity is a leadership choice, not a windfall. How it is directed determines whether AI acceleration translates into organizational outcomes or just faster backlogs.

Carry Forward Use the OnePacs model as a reference for how other teams frame their AI adoption narrative. Each team’s AI experiment summary for Q3 should include not just “what we automated” but “how we redirected found capacity and what that produced.” This framing connects individual team AI work to the company-level AI strategy.
