## General Facilitation Principles

**Set the evidence norm in the first 20 minutes and hold it all day.** The opening framing and the retrospective establish whether this onsite will be honest or performative. If vague claims go unchallenged in the retrospective, every subsequent session will be softer than it should be.

**You go first in every vulnerable exercise.** When sharing personal vision, when admitting a gap, when naming a behavior to stop. The team will calibrate their honesty to yours.

**Watch for these conversation patterns and intervene:**
- **Abstraction escape:** Someone moves from a specific issue to a general principle to avoid accountability. Redirect: "Let's stay concrete. What's the specific example?"
- **False alignment:** Everyone nods but nobody commits. Test: "So who's going to own this? By when?"
- **Blame displacement:** Attributing problems to IT, Product, Architecture, or "the business." Redirect: "What's within our control that we haven't done?"
- **Activity narration:** Describing what they did rather than what changed. Redirect: "What's different now because of that work?"
- **Hero stories:** Celebrating individual saves rather than systemic improvements. Redirect: "How do we make sure that doesn't require a hero next time?"

## Session-by-Session Facilitator Notes

### Opening: Why "Sharpen the Standard"

**Message injection point:** This is where you introduce "Excellence is expected, and the expectation keeps rising." Frame it as a natural evolution: Q124 asked "who are we?" Q224 defined excellence. Q324 focused. Q125 moved forward. Q425 recalibrated. Q2 2026 sharpens. The standard is not new. The rigor of holding it is.

**Tone check:** Confident but not triumphant. Honest but not self-flagellating. The frame is: we've built something real, and now we make it sharper.

---

### Retrospective: Evidence of Progress

**This is the most important facilitation moment of the onsite.**

**Hidden evidence standard:** The visible agenda says "wins, growth, and progress." The facilitation standard is: every win must be backed by evidence. Not "we improved" but "here's the metric / the before and after / the specific example."

**How to push gently without making it feel like an interrogation:**
- After a vague win: "That's great. Help me understand the scale. How much did that change? What's the number?"
- After a narrative win: "I want to make sure we capture this well. What would someone outside this room see as the proof?"
- After a qualitative win: "If you had to show Saji a single data point from this win, what would it be?"
- If someone genuinely has no metric: "That's okay. What would we need to measure to know this is working? Let's capture that as a gap."

**Do not:**
- Reject wins that are genuinely qualitative (some culture wins are real but hard to quantify)
- Make people feel punished for honesty about gaps
- Let the evidence standard become a gotcha. It's a muscle you're building, not a test you're administering.

**Watch for:**
- People who front-load their biggest win to avoid scrutiny on smaller claims
- People who describe their team's wins but not their own leadership contribution
- People who only present wins and skip growth/progress areas that aren't yet wins

**Message injection point (at the close of the retrospective):** "We will not confuse activity with value. The wins on this wall are real because they have evidence behind them. That's the standard we hold going forward."

### Continue / Start / Stop

**Push past the safe answers.** The easy Continue items are "keep doing demos" and "keep meeting weekly." The easy Stop items are "stop going to unnecessary meetings." Push toward behavioral commitments that are harder and more specific.

**Prompts to inject if the exercise stays too safe:**
- "What's one thing we say we value but don't actually do consistently?"
- "What behavior would we be embarrassed to admit to Saji?"
- "What's one thing we tolerate that we shouldn't?"
- "Where do we confuse heroics with health?"
- "What would our teams say we should stop doing if they could be completely honest?"

**Scoping enforcement:** If someone posts an operational item (e.g., "Start using Jira better"), redirect: "That's an operational improvement. For this exercise, I want us to focus on our behaviors as leaders. What's the leadership behavior behind that?"

**Message injection point (during clustering):** "Culture is the sum total of shared habits and expectations. It has tremendous inertia. Culture strangles innovation in the crib. What we're doing right now is choosing which habits to protect and which to change."

### Q425 Artifact Adoption Check

**This session will be uncomfortable. That is the point.**

The Q425 onsite produced five specific success criteria:
1. Clarity: Operating Model v1.0 and decision matrix live in Teams
2. Focus: Scorecard v1.0 with owners, cadence, metric removal policy
3. Behavior: GAIN feedback scripts adopted by all leads
4. Contribution: Architecture loop in use
5. Momentum: 30-day AI experiments completed with readout

**For each one, ask the room to grade honestly:** Adopted / Partially adopted / Not adopted.

**If the answer is "not adopted" for most items:**
- Do not pile on shame. The goal is understanding, not punishment.
- Ask: "What got in the way? Was the artifact wrong, or did we not protect the follow-through?"
- Ask: "If we build new artifacts this week, what makes us think they'll stick when these didn't?"
- This is where you can inject: "We are trying to get it right, not to be right."

**If the answer is "mostly adopted":**
- Celebrate it briefly. Then ask: "What's the next level? What's the v2.0 need?"

### Personal Vision and Goals

**Day 1 (Drafting):**
- Distribute the blank BYB template and the example.
- Emphasize: this is not a performance review exercise. This is about who you want to be as a leader, not just what you want to accomplish.
- Prompt: "Write your vision as if you're describing the leader you want to be at the next onsite. Not what you'll have done, but who you'll have become."
- The "gap between intent and current behavior" question is the hardest. Model it yourself by sharing your own gap openly on Day 2.

**Day 2 (Sharing):**
- You go first. Share a vision that is honest about your gaps. Name the Saji feedback directly: "My gap is between setting direction and proving it with outcomes. That's what I'm working on."
- After each person shares, invite one piece of genuine challenge from the group. Not "that's great" but "have you thought about..." or "I notice you didn't mention..."
- If someone's vision is entirely about their team's execution and doesn't address their own leadership growth, gently redirect: "I hear a lot about what your team will do. What about you? What's the leadership behavior you're growing?"

**Message injection point (at the close of sharing):** "Reach that destination where the things that are pleasant and the things that are honorable, finally become, for you, the same." (Seneca)

### Whiteboard Goals: Hidden SMART Conversion

**The visible agenda says "Team Goals and Commitments." The word SMART never appears.**

Phase 1 (brainstorm) should feel open and energizing. Do not filter or critique during this phase. Volume matters.

Phase 3 (refinement) is where the SMART conversion happens naturally. The facilitator's questions do the work:

| SMART Element | Facilitator Question |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Specific | "Is this specific enough that someone new to the team would know exactly what to do?" |
| Measurable | "How would we know if we achieved this? What's the number or signal?" |
| Assignable | "Who owns making this happen? Not 'the team' -- a person." |
| Realistic | "Is this within our control or influence? Can we actually move this in one quarter?" |
| Time-bound | "By when? What's the deadline?" |

**If a goal can't survive these questions, it either needs to be reframed or moved to the parking lot.** Do not force bad goals into SMART format. Some aspirations are legitimate aspirations and shouldn't be forced into quarterly commitments.

**Watch for:**
- Goals that are really activities disguised as outcomes ("implement Datadog dashboards" vs. "reduce mean time to detect production issues from X to Y")
- Goals where ownership is diffuse ("the team will..." without a named person)
- Goals that are too easy (already happening, just claiming credit)
- Goals that are too ambitious (multi-quarter effort crammed into one quarter)

### Carry the Load

**Setup logistics:**
- Source weighted items in advance. Sandbags, loaded backpacks, water jugs, or similar.
- Total weight should be enough that no single person can comfortably carry it all, but manageable when distributed.
- Route should be 20-30 min of walking, preferably with some variation (flat section, slight incline, uneven ground).
- Bring water.

**Facilitation during the walk:**
- Start silently. Let the team figure out distribution on their own.
- Observe who picks up weight first, who waits, who notices others struggling, who asks for help versus who suffers silently.
- Do not intervene unless someone is at genuine physical risk.
- Halfway through, if the distribution has become static, you can prompt: "Is this the right distribution for the second half?"

**Debrief prompts:**
- "What did you notice about how we distributed the load?"
- "Who noticed when someone was struggling before they said anything?"
- "Did anyone carry more than they should have? Why?"
- "Where in our real work do we see the same pattern: someone carrying too much while others could help but don't notice?"
- "What does 'Team First' mean to you after that walk?"

**Do not:**
- Over-interpret the exercise. It's a physical metaphor, not a personality assessment.
- Make it competitive or timed. The point is collective, not individual.
- Skip it if weather is imperfect. Light rain or cold is fine. Only cancel for genuinely dangerous conditions.

### Commitments and Close

**The "one thing" commitment must be specific enough to verify at the next check-in.** If someone says "I'll focus more on metrics," push: "What specifically will you do in the next 30 days?"

**The "one behavior to stop" should be a real behavior, not a platitude.** If someone says "stop being reactive," push: "What specific reactive pattern are you going to change? What's the trigger?"

**The accountability partner selection is important.** Pairs should not be obvious allies. Encourage people to pair with someone who will genuinely challenge them. The accountability partner's job is one check-in per week for 30 days: "Did you do the thing you committed to?"

**Closing message:** "If an individual's vision does not align with and contribute to collective goals, that is a problem. We leave here aligned. The work is proving it."

## Signals to Watch Throughout the Onsite

| Signal | What It Looks Like | How to Respond |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| Defensiveness | Arms crossed, deflecting questions, "that's not fair" responses | Acknowledge the discomfort, reframe as learning, move on |
| Abstraction | Moving from specific to general to avoid accountability | "Let's stay concrete. What's the specific example?" |
| Avoidance | Silence on hard topics, changing the subject, checking phones | Call it gently: "I notice we moved past that quickly. Is there something there worth exploring?" |
| False alignment | Quick agreement without engagement | "Before we move on, does anyone see this differently?" |
| Hero framing | Celebrating individual saves over systemic solutions | "How do we make sure that doesn't need a hero next time?" |
| Energy collapse | Low participation, side conversations, fidgeting | Call a break, shift format, move to physical activity |
| Genuine breakthrough | Someone names a real gap honestly, offers a specific commitment, challenges a peer constructively | Acknowledge it. "Thank you for that. That's exactly the kind of honesty we need." |
