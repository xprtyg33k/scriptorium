Full Onsite Summary: Product-To-Engineering Process

The two-day onsite produced a clear first version of the handoff model: Product creates a structured, plain-language artifact packet; Engineering and Architecture validate it through a cold read; Engineering then owns TRD creation and delivery decomposition. The model is promising because it surfaced hidden assumptions early, before they became sprint churn.

Defined Artifact packet, review method, lane split, and validation standard.
Proved Cold readers can expose missing context better than internal review alone.
Refine Glossary, README, source links, visual references, and TRD trigger need formalization.

Executive Report Out

We should move forward with this operating model as a pilot standard. The onsite showed that better product artifacts do not slow engineering down; they remove ambiguity earlier and make engineering autonomy safer. The process is not ready to be declared final, but it is strong enough to run again with a hardened packet checklist and formal TRD handoff.

Product Process: Before And After

The biggest change is not a new document. It is a new operating contract between Product, Engineering, Architecture, QA, and AI-assisted execution.

Before: Scattered Context

Relevant context lived in meetings, individual memory, drafts, personal spaces, chat links, and partial artifacts.

Observed Problem

Engineers and agents fill gaps with assumptions, causing back-and-forth, rework, and inconsistent interpretation.

After: Product Intelligence Hub

A shared location holds the packet, glossary, decisions, source links, review history, and handoff state.

Before: PRD As Catch-All

The PRD risked absorbing strategy, business rules, technical design, implementation details, and metrics all at once.

Observed Problem

Product could become overly technical, while Engineering either challenged the PRD or treated accidental wording as a requirement.

After: Layered Artifacts

Strategy holds context, PRD holds product outcomes, design/workflow artifacts show experience, and TRD holds implementation details.

Before: Engineering Waits Or Fills Gaps

Engineering often entered after Product believed the work was ready, then pushed back on missing or unclear detail.

Observed Problem

The feedback loop happened too late, creating tension between "not enough process" and "too much process."

After: Cold Validation Gate

Engineering validates the packet before sprint or build commitment, with gaps treated as process output, not failure.

Before: Completion Bias

Requirements could be treated as something to finish before Engineering begins.

Observed Problem

AI-assisted build compresses implementation, but product discovery and context gathering are not equally compressible.

After: Slice And Learn

The process supports early experiments, spikes, and small build-to-learn slices while maintaining artifact discipline.

Defined Operating Model

This is the proposed Product-to-Engineering process as it stands after the onsite.

Signal Product gathers customer signal, market context, business goals, decision rationale, constraints, and known workflow evidence.
Context Product curates the packet: README, strategy, module PRD, glossary, dependency map, workflows, design references, assumptions, and open questions.
Decision Product states what is in, out, later, unknown, and why. Open questions are routed with owners and impact.
Validation Engineering and Architecture cold-read the packet, comment inline, classify gaps, and determine whether it is ready for TRD generation.
Execution Engineering creates TRD, JIRA artifacts, QA inputs, implementation plan, and agent context, then writes lessons back to memory.
Artifact Structure

Core Packet

  • Handoff README: entry point, reading order, artifact purpose, review questions, feedback channel.
  • Strategy or solution context: source of truth for market, customer, value, roadmap, and broad constraints.
  • Module PRD: plain-language product requirements for one module or slice.
  • Glossary and dependency map: named systems, terms, users, states, dependencies, and canonical source links.
  • Workflow/design references: Figma, screens, flows, mockups, journey diagrams, sequence-like product flows.

Engineering Add-On

  • TRD: implementation route, technical decisions, standards, interfaces, system boundaries, data, events, and compliance implementation.
  • Delivery breakdown: epics, stories, spikes, QA scenarios, acceptance test plan, and agent prompts.
  • Architecture review record: decisions, tradeoffs, rejected options, and unresolved technical risks.
Communication Process And Channels

Channel Rules

  • Meeting chat is for distribution only: links, timing, and quick logistics.
  • Inline comments are for artifact-specific feedback and resolution history.
  • Shared wiki/folder is the durable source of truth.
  • Architecture Hub is the durable system and standards reference.
  • JIRA is generated after artifact validation, not used as the initial product definition container.

Feedback Rules

  • Every comment should trace to a section, term, rule, dependency, or missing source.
  • Every open question needs audience, owner, impact, and disposition.
  • Every live answer that changes understanding must be written back to the artifact.

Proposed Engineering Process

Engineering's role becomes more active, not less. The goal is autonomy with shared context.

Step 1 Receive

Receive The Validated Product Packet

Engineering starts from the handoff README and confirms the packet contains the expected artifacts, source links, glossary, dependency map, and open-question routing.

Step 2 Read

Run A Cold Read And Comment Pass

At least one reader with limited domain context reads the packet and comments before live explanation. Questions are classified as product gap, definition gap, dependency gap, contradiction, or TRD input.

Step 3 TRD

Create The Technical Requirements Document

Engineering and Architecture translate the product packet into the technical route: systems, contracts, events, services, data, compliance implementation, non-functional requirements, and architecture tradeoffs.

Step 4 Plan

Generate Work And Quality Artifacts

Use PRD plus TRD to generate epics, stories, spikes, QA scenarios, acceptance checks, and agent prompts. Trace each item back to source sections.

Step 5 Build

Build With Feedback Loops

Use spikes and early slices when discovery cannot be completed through documents alone. Capture decisions and lessons back into the shared memory layer.

Engineering's general ask is clear: do not prescribe implementation in the PRD, but do provide enough business rule, dependency, vocabulary, workflow, and rationale context that Engineering does not have to guess.

Lessons Learned

The repeatable lessons are process lessons, not telehealth-specific conclusions.

Clarity Is Not Ceremony

The packet must earn its place by reducing ambiguity, enabling decisions, or supporting execution. Documentation is valuable when it prevents avoidable rework.

Cold Readers Are Essential

People with less domain knowledge surface hidden assumptions faster than the team that created the artifacts.

Plain Language Is Hard

Product must be precise without becoming technical. Engineering is literal, and AI agents are even more literal.

Shorter Is Not Always Better

The MVP artifact pack did not automatically remove questions. The right test is whether the reader can answer the review questions, not page count.

AI Changes The Cost Model

Build is more compressible than product discovery. The process must preserve customer signal and context while enabling smaller experiments and faster slices.

Context Needs Architecture

The process needs a knowledge architecture: glossary, cross-links, source ownership, artifact order, decision log, and explicit references.

Action Items And Follow-Up Activities

These actions convert the onsite into a usable organizational process.

Publish The Packet Standard

Create a reusable checklist for README, strategy, PRD, glossary, dependency map, design/workflow links, assumptions, open questions, and review questions.

  • Owner area: Product Ops / Product leadership.
  • Output: standard packet template and quality gate.

Define The TRD Standard

Clarify when TRD work starts, who owns it, what inputs it consumes, and how it hands off to delivery planning.

  • Owner area: Engineering and Architecture.
  • Output: TRD template plus architecture review workflow.

Create PRD Language Guardrails

Build AI generation rails that avoid implementation-coded terms and force plain-language outcomes.

  • Owner area: Product, with Engineering review.
  • Output: prompt instructions, "do not use" glossary, example rewrites.

Run A TRD/JIRA Dry Run

Use the revised packet to generate a TRD outline, sample JIRA epics/stories, QA scenarios, and agent prompts.

  • Owner area: Engineering lead plus Product partner.
  • Output: traceable source-to-work map and missing-context list.

Pilot The Model On One More Initiative

Repeat the process with a different initiative and at least one cold reader outside the core domain.

  • Owner area: Product and Engineering leadership.
  • Output: validation of whether the pattern generalizes beyond telehealth.

Finalize Communication Channels

Decide the durable source of truth, naming/versioning rules, comment workflow, and when JIRA becomes the execution container.

  • Owner area: Product Ops, Engineering Ops, and Architecture.
  • Output: channel map and artifact lifecycle policy.
Executive Summary Message

Recommended Message

The onsite successfully produced and tested a first version of the Product-to-Engineering handoff model. The process is valuable because it exposed implicit assumptions, unclear vocabulary, and implementation-risking language before the team moved into build. We recommend adopting the model as a pilot standard, with immediate refinement to the artifact packet, TRD handoff, source-of-truth structure, and AI-generation guardrails.

Decision Ask

Approve one more structured pilot using the revised packet standard, with Engineering and Architecture accountable for the TRD path and Product accountable for the source packet quality. The success measure should be whether the validated packet can produce useful TRD direction, JIRA work items, QA checks, and AI-agent context with materially less rework.
